John Conway's Game of Life

Generation Count: 0

The Game of Life, also known simply as Life, is a cellular automaton devised by the British mathematician John Horton Conway in 1970. It is a zero-player game, meaning that its evolution is determined by its initial state, requiring no further input. One interacts with the Game of Life by creating an initial configuration and observing how it evolves. It is Turing complete and can simulate a universal constructor or any other Turing machine.

  • Any live cell with fewer than two live neighbors dies, as if by underpopulation.
  • Any live cell with two or three live neighbors lives on to the next generation.
  • Any live cell with more than three live neighbors dies, as if by overpopulation.
  • Any dead cell with exactly three live neighbors becomes a live cell, as if by reproduction.

Well first of all I think it's just really cool, and it also has the perk of being interactive. You can spend hours playing with it. I also wanted to show potential employers that at this point I have the ability to pick up and use new programming languages quite easily. I haven't used Javscript in a couple years and never extensively, but I was able to pick it up and quickly create this with a little help from Google.

I'd have to say that efficiency was the biggest challenge. In my first iterations of coding this I was using multiple two dimensional arrays. One was for the current generation of cells, another was for the next generation, one was for tracking the cell's neighbor counts... there was a lot of looping. I ended up replacing all of those with a single three dimensional array. Then I used another 3D array to create a mapping of every cell with it's possible coordinates. Previously I had been calculating that on every loop. then I realized frontloading that work into an initial mapping would help greatly, which it did. I also coded and hosted this whole website on a Raspberry Pi 400, so there was that limitation as well. (Yes I have better computers, but after working at a Windows only job for almost 2 years, I wanted to get back in touch with Linux).

Try hitting 'Randomize' and then 'Auto Advance'. Or just click 'Preset'. Watch cool things happen.

You can click on cells to toggle them, 'Advance Gen' will move the simulation forward one generation of cells. It's fully interactive. Play around with the controls, they're there for a reason. Have fun.
